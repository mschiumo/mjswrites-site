Why I still write on a typewriter

On ribbons, wobbly letters, and the quiet case for slowing down.

Michael J. Schiuma June 2026 4 min read

There's a teal Smith-Corona on my desk that's older than I am, and I write nearly everything on it first. In a house full of screens, it's the one machine that asks something of me. It's slow. It's loud. It has opinions about the letter e. And I wouldn't trade it for anything.

A keyboard is built for efficiency; a typewriter is built for commitment. There is no delete key, no cursor to slip back and soften a line you meant. Every word is a small act of faith — you press, and it's there, inked and permanent. It makes you mean what you say.

A typewriter doesn't let you take anything back. Neither, really, does life.

There's a ritual to it, too. Rolling in a fresh sheet. The clack and return. The faint smell of ribbon ink. When I type a custom poem for someone, that whole ceremony becomes part of the gift — proof that a real person sat with their story and pressed it, letter by letter, into the page.

And the imperfections are the point. A letter that sits a hair too high. A word that ghosts where the ribbon ran thin. Those little flaws are the fingerprints of a human being trying — which is, I think, the most honest thing a poem can be.

So I'll keep the old machine. In a world that's forever asking me to go faster, it's the thing that lets me slow down enough to actually mean it.

— Michael
